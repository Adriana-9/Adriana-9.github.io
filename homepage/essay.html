My New York Essay

Towards the very top of Manhattan, from 155th Street to Dyckman Street, is a neighborhood with a history deeply rooted there. Washington Heights, a place that is considered home to many and where the majority of things are in Spanish like: the store signs, the music being played and almost every one around you is speaking in Spanish. The majority of the businesses here weren’t owned by big companies, or well, that's how I remembered it. Neighbors getting along with each other like if they are a family. In a type of way they are. A strong connection that has been made among each other over the years that feels like nothing can break it.

The way my neighbor, who is an old Dominican lady, considers me as one of her granddaughters. She has been living in the same apartment across from my apartment for over 16 years. She had seen me when I was a couple days old back during those cold January days in 2009 and till right now, present day. Her tired voice asks me each day how my day was when I get back home from school. Almost every day I see her sitting outside her apartment with her grey hair in a ponytail. She sees and talks to neighbors who walk in and out of the 100-year-old building. There is always someone like her for everyone in each building in this city. Or an old man in the nearest bodega store, making sure you are okay. For me it’s an old Dominican man who started to consider me as one of his nieces on a random day while I was ordering food. Each time I cross paths with him, he has a warm smile on his face, wears a pattern button up shirt, a hat, and asks how I have been. He always emphasizes how I have to focus on school, and if there is anyone bothering me, I can tell anyone in the bodega, and they will help me. There is at least one person in this big city who will care about you, even when you think there isn’t.

The stores start to close left and right after some time. They pack up and leave. New, bigger stores come and unpack. Chick-fil-a replacing a bank, Target replacing Modell’s Sporting Goods, Foot Locker replacing a kids store. The stores we saw while walking the streets below people’s eye level are all different, while we walk those same streets but this time closer or above, or even at the same eye level of others. But this isn’t the only change; it’s not only about stores but the people as well. Little by little, the Hispanic community is decreasing in the Heights. The streets that I ran around when I was little, the same streets that I saw familiar faces on, now had new ones. Faces that would give me a little smile or just people that are in their own world, they don’t even look my way, which I’m fine with, have changed to those who look at me with a face of confusion, as if I’m in the wrong place. Faces that have eyes that I feel judge me, and when I turn to look at them, I’m correct, they have a judgemental facial expression like if I did something to them in my own neighborhood that I've been in for way longer than them. It makes me feel out of place when I shouldn't. What used to be a rare encounter started to become a bit more common.

Change can be something that you never fully notice at first, but slowly it becomes so noticeable that you question yourself how it happened. It can feel like you are on a ride that never stops, even when you want it to, you feel the wind blowing by you and you see the surroundings are different each time. A ride that you wish had taken its time, but clearly it didn’t because time doesn’t wait for anyone. Especially in this city, you just have to adapt to it. Adapt to the surroundings and to the people that come and go as well. The streets that I know way too well hold my memories, they hold my actions and my words that linger around like a ghost. It knows me way too well, a place that has been my home for 16 years, despite the transformation it has undergone and is still going through now, it doesn’t change in my heart.
